## Context

Azure charges reflect architecture and operating decisions made across subscriptions, resource groups and teams. Costs become difficult to control when resources lack owners, budgets do not reflect expected usage, or technical teams treat the monthly invoice as a finance problem.

Cost optimisation is ongoing operational work. It should protect reliability, security and delivery commitments while removing spend that does not support a business outcome.

## Why It Matters

Poor cost visibility delays decisions. An unused disk, oversized database or continuously running test environment might look minor in isolation, but repeated gaps accumulate across an estate.

The reverse problem also matters. A rushed cost reduction that ignores performance, recovery or licensing constraints creates operational risk. Every recommendation needs an owner, evidence and a rollback path where the change affects service capacity.

## Risks and Considerations

### Discounts can preserve waste

Reservations and savings plans reduce eligible rates in exchange for a one-year or three-year commitment. They work best for stable, understood demand. Rightsizing or shutdown changes that demand, so calculate commitments after those changes have settled.

### Low utilisation needs context

A quiet virtual machine might provide standby capacity, support a monthly process or meet a recovery requirement. Review utilisation over a period that covers the workload cycle and speak with the owner before changing it.

### Storage tiers have access costs

Moving data to cool, cold or archive tiers changes retrieval cost and availability. Confirm access patterns, retention obligations and recovery expectations before changing lifecycle rules.

### Allocation is not optimisation

Tags and cost centres explain who pays. They do not reduce spend by themselves. Use allocation data to create ownership and prompt decisions.

## Recommended Approach

1. **Establish the baseline.** Review actual and amortised cost, major service categories, recent changes and forecast demand.
2. **Assign ownership.** Record an owner, environment, service and cost centre for each material resource group or workload.
3. **Remove obvious waste.** Investigate unattached disks, old snapshots, unused public IP addresses, idle services and superseded test resources.
4. **Schedule non-production workloads.** Deallocate eligible virtual machines and other resources outside required hours. Confirm that shutdown automation results in the billable state you expect.
5. **Rightsize active services.** Use Azure Advisor, platform metrics and workload knowledge. Change one controlled group at a time and monitor performance.
6. **Review architecture and licensing.** Check Azure Hybrid Benefit, service tiers, scaling rules and whether managed services reduce total operating cost.
7. **Assess commitments.** Compare reservations and savings plans only after demand is stable. Review scope, utilisation, term, exchange limits and ownership.
8. **Measure realised savings.** Record the recommendation, approved action, expected saving and observed cost after implementation.

Microsoft recommends applying Azure Advisor actions in this order: rightsizing or shutdown, then reservations, then savings plans. Each action changes the demand used to calculate the next recommendation.

## Practical Next Steps

Run a focused review of your largest cost drivers:

* identify the workload owner and business purpose
* compare the last three complete billing periods
* review Azure Advisor recommendations and utilisation data
* check whether existing reservations or savings plans are fully used
* agree one low-risk action and measure the result
