## Context

The Essential Eight addresses eight areas:

1. application control
2. patch applications
3. configure Microsoft Office macro settings
4. user application hardening
5. restrict administrative privileges
6. patch operating systems
7. multi-factor authentication
8. regular backups

Each area has requirements for Maturity Levels One, Two and Three. An organisation should implement one maturity level consistently across all eight areas before moving to the next level.

The Essential Eight is mandatory in some Australian Government contexts. Other organisations often adopt it as a practical baseline or because contracts, insurers, boards or regulators expect evidence of stronger controls. Confirm which obligations apply to your organisation rather than describing every implementation as "compliance".

## Why It Matters

Security settings drift. Devices fall outside management, emergency accounts avoid normal controls, applications stop receiving updates, and backup jobs complete without anyone testing recovery.

An Essential Eight assessment gives leaders a clearer view of those gaps. It also creates a basis for funding decisions, remediation priorities and later reassessment.

## Risks and Considerations

### Maturity is not a product feature

Microsoft Intune, Microsoft Defender, Microsoft Entra ID, Windows Update for Business and related services support many controls. Their presence does not prove that requirements are configured, enforced and operating across the assessed scope.

### Scope changes the result

Define the systems, users, devices, cloud services and administrative paths covered by the assessment. Record exclusions and their risk treatment. A narrow assessment presented as an organisation-wide result will mislead decision-makers.

### Evidence matters

Policy documents and screenshots are weak evidence by themselves. Collect device inventory, configuration exports, vulnerability data, sign-in evidence, privileged-role records, patch reports, backup results and recovery test records.

### Maturity needs ongoing work

Assessment records a point in time. Ownership, reporting and regular review reduce drift after remediation. Reassess after material environment changes and at an agreed interval.

### The Essential Eight is a baseline

It does not address every cyber risk. Organisations still need controls appropriate to their environment, such as security monitoring, incident response, supplier risk, identity lifecycle management and protection for non-Microsoft platforms.

## Recommended Approach

1. **Confirm obligations and risk.** Identify contractual, government, regulatory, insurance and board requirements.
2. **Choose the target maturity level.** Base the target on the threats and business consequences relevant to the organisation.
3. **Define assessment scope.** Include users, devices, applications, cloud services, administrative systems and recovery platforms.
4. **Assess all eight areas.** Record the requirement, current state, evidence, gap, risk owner and recommended treatment.
5. **Prioritise blocking controls.** Address gaps that prevent consistent maturity across an area before polishing controls that already exceed the target.
6. **Implement and test.** Confirm that settings reach the intended scope and test operational outcomes, including patch deployment, privileged access and data recovery.
7. **Report clearly.** Separate implemented, partially implemented, planned, excluded and not applicable controls.
8. **Monitor for drift.** Assign owners, review exceptions and repeat evidence collection.

## Practical Next Steps

Ask your IT and security teams for four items:

* the target Essential Eight maturity level and why it was selected
* the systems and users included in the assessment
* the evidence supporting each assessed control
* the remediation plan, owners and review dates for open gaps
